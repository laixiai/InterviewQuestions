## 10 Typical Advertising Solutions Architect, gTech Ads, Large Customer Sales Interview Questions

### Question 1ï¼šDescribe a time you worked with a large customer who had a clear business objective but no idea how to achieve it technically. How did you approach the situation?
*   **Points of Assessment**: This question evaluates your consultative skills, problem-solving process, and ability to translate business needs into a technical roadmap. The interviewer wants to see how you structure discovery, manage client expectations, and take ownership of a solution.
*   **Standard Answer**: "In my previous role, a major e-commerce client wanted to increase their customer lifetime value by 20% but was only focused on acquisition campaigns. I started by scheduling a series of deep-dive workshops with their marketing and BI teams to understand their customer journey and data infrastructure. I identified that they weren't effectively utilizing their first-party data for remarketing or audience segmentation. I then architected a multi-phase solution. Phase one involved implementing advanced conversion tracking and integrating their CRM data into Google Ads. Phase two focused on building dynamic remarketing campaigns and predictive audience lists. I presented this roadmap with clear milestones and projected ROI, which gained their buy-in and ultimately helped them exceed their goal within nine months."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Giving a purely technical answer without linking it back to the business objective. Failing to mention the collaborative or "consultative" part of the process.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   How did you handle any pushback from the client's technical team?
    *   What metrics did you use to measure the success of your proposed solution?
    *   How did you ensure the project stayed on track across multiple quarters?

### Question 2ï¼šImagine a key client's campaign performance suddenly drops due to a technical issue with their tracking implementation. Walk me through your troubleshooting process.
*   **Points of Assessment**: This assesses your technical troubleshooting skills, logical thinking under pressure, and client communication abilities. The interviewer wants to see a systematic and calm approach.
*   **Standard Answer**: "My immediate priority would be to acknowledge the issue with the client and assure them I'm investigating. I would first work to isolate the variable: was it a change on their website, in their Google Tag Manager container, or within the Google Ads platform? I would use tools like Google Analytics' real-time view and the Tag Assistant to check for firing errors. I'd then examine the change history in their accounts to correlate the performance drop with any recent modifications. Concurrently, I'd communicate transparently with the client, providing regular updates on my findings. Once the root cause is identifiedâ€”for instance, a developer accidentally removed a dataLayer variableâ€”I would provide clear instructions for the fix and then monitor performance closely to confirm the issue is resolved."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Jumping to conclusions without a structured investigation. Forgetting the importance of client communication during the crisis.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   What if the issue was not on the client's side, but a suspected Google product bug?
    *   How would you explain the technical problem to a non-technical marketing manager?
    *   What steps would you take to prevent such an issue from happening again?

### Question 3ï¼šHow would you design a technical solution to help a mobile gaming app developer increase user acquisition and retention?
*   **Points of Assessment**: This question tests your knowledge of the app advertising ecosystem, your system design capabilities, and your ability to think strategically about a client's full lifecycle.
*   **Standard Answer**: "I would propose an integrated solution focused on three pillars. For acquisition, I'd architect the implementation of Google App Campaigns, ensuring they have robust conversion tracking set up via Firebase to optimize for valuable in-app actions, not just installs. For retention, I would help them integrate their app analytics to create audience segments of lapsed users or non-spenders. We could then use these lists for targeted re-engagement campaigns with special offers. The third pillar is measurement: I'd ensure they have a solid analytics and attribution model in place to understand which channels drive the most valuable users, allowing them to allocate their budget effectively. This holistic approach connects acquisition with long-term value."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Focusing only on user acquisition and ignoring retention. Proposing a generic solution without mentioning specific Google products like Firebase or App Campaigns.
*   **Potential Follow--up Questions**:
    *   How would you advise the client on handling user privacy regulations like ATT?
    *   What kind of proof of concept could you build to demonstrate the value of this approach?
    *   How would you measure the incremental impact of the re-engagement campaigns?

*   **Points of Assessment**: Tests your collaboration, communication, and influencing skills. The interviewer wants to see that you can build consensus and drive action across different groups with potentially different priorities.

### Question 7ï¼šHow would you explain the concept of a complex technical system, like a data clean room, to a non-technical Chief Marketing Officer?
*   **Points of Assessment**: This directly evaluates your ability to distill complex topics for a non-technical audience, a core requirement of the job. The focus is on clarity, analogy, and business relevance.
*   **Standard Answer**: "I would use an analogy. I'd say, 'Imagine you and Google both have valuable customer lists, but for privacy reasons, you can't just exchange them. *   **Common Pitfalls**: Using technical jargon (e.g., "hashing," "APIs") in the explanation. Failing to connect the concept to a tangible business outcome.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   How would you answer a follow-up question from the CMO about the cost-benefit analysis?

*   **Common Pitfalls**: Describing a project that was purely theoretical and never built. Being unable to clearly explain the purpose or the outcome of the proof of concept.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   What was the most challenging part of building that PoC?
    *   How do you decide when a proof of concept is necessary versus just presenting an idea?
    *   How would you scale that solution from a PoC to a full production system?

*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   How do you handle difficult questions or feedback from a client during a QBR?
    *   What's the ideal balance between reviewing the past and planning for the future in a QBR?
