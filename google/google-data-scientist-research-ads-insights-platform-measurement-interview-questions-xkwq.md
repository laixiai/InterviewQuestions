## Mastering Causal Inference in Advertising
Beyond standard A/B testing, this research-focused role demands a sophisticated command of causal inference techniques to answer critical business questions. You will frequently encounter scenarios where randomized controlled trials are not feasible, requiring you to employ quasi-experimental methods. This includes techniques like difference-in-differences, instrumental variables, and regression discontinuity to isolate the causal impact of advertising from confounding factors. For example, you might be asked to measure the true lift in sales from a video ad campaign, accounting for selection biases where users who see the ad are already more likely to purchase. A deep understanding of the underlying assumptions of each method and the ability to diagnose potential biases is crucial. This expertise is what separates correlation-based observations from true causal insights, allowing Google and its advertisers to make multi-million dollar decisions with confidence. Your ability to apply these advanced methods will be a key driver of your impact on the team.

### Question 1ï¼šImagine we want to measure the incremental lift in product sales from a new YouTube video ad campaign. How would you design an experiment to measure this, and what are the key metrics you would look at?
*   **Points of Assessment**: This question assesses your understanding of experimental design, causal inference, and practical business metrics. The interviewer wants to see if you can design a scientifically valid test and identify relevant success indicators.
*   **Standard Answer**: To measure the incremental lift, I would design a randomized controlled trial (RCT), also known as an A/B test. I would randomly assign YouTube users into two groups: a treatment group that is eligible to see the new video ad, and a control group that is not. It is crucial that the assignment is random to eliminate selection bias. The primary metric would be the conversion rate (e.g., product purchases) for both groups over a defined period. The incremental lift would be calculated as the difference in the conversion rate between the treatment and control groups. I would also analyze secondary metrics like website visits, ad recall, and cost per incremental conversion to get a fuller picture of the campaign's impact.
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Failing to specify a randomized control group. Suggesting a simple before-and-after analysis, which doesn't control for seasonality or other external factors. Forgetting to mention the importance of statistical significance when interpreting the results.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   What if you cannot run a perfectly randomized experiment? What alternative methods would you consider?
    *   How would you determine the necessary sample size for this experiment?
    *   What potential biases or confounding variables might you encounter, and how would you address them?

### Question 2ï¼šWhat is causal inference, and why is it more valuable than correlation when analyzing ad effectiveness? Please provide an example.
*   **Points of Assessment**: This tests your fundamental understanding of a core concept for this role. The interviewer is checking if you can articulate the difference between correlation and causation and apply it to an advertising context.
*   **Standard Answer**: Causal inference is the process of determining if an action or event is the direct cause of an outcome. It goes beyond correlation, which simply indicates that two variables move together without explaining why. In advertising, this distinction is critical. For example, we might observe a correlation between users who see luxury car ads and users who buy luxury cars. However, this could be because the ads are targeted at high-income individuals who were already likely to buy such a car (selection bias). Causal inference methods, like A/B testing, help us isolate the true effect of the ad itself by creating a comparable control group. This allows us to measure the *incremental* impactâ€”the sales that happened *only because* of the adâ€”which is the true measure of ROI.
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Giving a purely academic definition without a practical example. Confusing confounding variables with instrumental variables. Unable to clearly articulate the business value of causation over correlation.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   Can you describe a statistical technique, other than A/B testing, used for causal inference?
    *   What are the main assumptions behind the difference-in-differences method?
    *   How would you explain the concept of a "confounder" to a product manager?
