## 10 Typical Account Strategist, SBS Engage, Google Customer Solutions (Thai, English) Interview Questions

### Question 1ï¼šDescribe a time you managed a portfolio of clients. How did you prioritize their needs and identify opportunities for growth?
*   **Points of Assessment**: This question evaluates your portfolio management skills, strategic thinking, and ability to prioritize in a fast-paced environment. The interviewer wants to see if you have a systematic approach to driving growth across multiple accounts.
*   **Standard Answer**: "In my previous role, I managed a portfolio of 50 small business clients. To prioritize, I segmented them into tiers based on revenue potential and strategic importance. I conducted quarterly business reviews with my top-tier clients to deeply understand their goals and challenges. For growth, I analyzed their performance data to identify trends and untapped opportunities. For example, I noticed one e-commerce client had a high click-through rate but a low conversion rate. By analyzing their user journey in Google Analytics, I recommended landing page optimizations that led to a 20% increase in conversions over the next quarter. This data-driven, tiered approach allowed me to efficiently allocate my time and consistently drive incremental growth across my entire portfolio."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Giving a generic answer without a clear methodology. Failing to provide a specific example with measurable results. Focusing only on reactive problem-solving instead of proactive growth identification.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   How would you handle a situation where multiple high-priority clients have urgent needs simultaneously?
    *   What tools or methods do you use to track and manage your client portfolio?
    *   How do you define a "growth opportunity" for a client?

### Question 2ï¼šWalk me through how you would advise a client who is new to Google Ads and has a limited budget.
*   **Points of Assessment**: Assesses your understanding of fundamental Google Ads principles, your consultative skills, and your ability to strategize for clients with constraints. The interviewer is looking for a thoughtful, client-centric approach.
*   **Standard Answer**: "First, I would focus on understanding their core business objectivesâ€”are they seeking leads, online sales, or brand awareness? For a client with a limited budget, maximizing ROI is key. I would recommend starting with a tightly-themed Search campaign, focusing on long-tail keywords with high purchase intent to control costs and attract qualified traffic. We would set up conversion tracking from day one to measure what's working. I'd also emphasize the importance of a compelling landing page experience. To manage the budget effectively, I would set a daily cap and use manual bidding initially to gain insights before potentially testing automated strategies. My goal would be to secure some early wins and demonstrate the value of the investment, building their confidence to scale in the future."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Suggesting overly complex strategies unsuitable for a beginner. Forgetting to mention foundational steps like conversion tracking. Not asking clarifying questions about the client's business first.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   How would you explain the concept of Quality Score to this client?
    *   What key metrics would you tell this client to focus on in their first month?
    *   At what point would you recommend they expand into Display or YouTube campaigns?

### Question 3ï¼šTell me about a time a client was skeptical about your recommendations. How did you handle their objections?
*   **Points of Assessment**: This behavioral question gauges your objection handling skills, resilience, and ability to build trust. The interviewer wants to see how you use data and persuasion to influence clients.
*   **Standard Answer**: "I worked with a client who was hesitant to increase their budget for a campaign I proposed, as their previous marketing efforts had yielded poor results. I started by acknowledging their concerns to show I was listening. Then, instead of just pushing my idea, I presented a detailed forecast based on market data and competitor analysis, showing the potential return on ad spend. I also suggested a two-week trial run with a smaller, controlled budget focused on their most profitable service. I committed to daily monitoring and a weekly performance review. The trial exceeded the projected KPIs, and the client gained the confidence to approve the full budget. This experience taught me the importance of backing up recommendations with data and using a phased approach to build trust."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Being defensive or dismissive of the client's concerns. Failing to provide data or evidence to support your position. Not offering a compromise or a trial to mitigate the client's risk.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   What would you do if the client still refused your recommendation after you presented the data?
    *   How do you differentiate between a valid client concern and simple resistance to change?
    *   Describe another sales technique you find effective.

### Question 4ï¼šHow would you use storytelling to build a compelling narrative for a client sales pitch?
*   **Points of Assessment**: This tests a preferred qualification. It assesses your communication skills and your ability to translate features and data into a persuasive and engaging client presentation.
*   **Standard Answer**: "Storytelling is crucial for making data relatable. I would start a pitch by framing the client's current situation as the beginning of a storyâ€”highlighting their business goals and the challenges they face. Then, I would introduce Google's advertising solutions not as products, but as the 'guide' or 'tool' that will help them overcome these challenges. I'd use a case study of a similar business as the plot, showing how they went from point A to point B using a specific strategy. I would weave in data points as 'milestones' in the story. The climax would be the projected outcome for the client's own business, painting a clear picture of their future success. This narrative structure makes the pitch more memorable and emotionally resonant than just listing features."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Confusing storytelling with just talking a lot. Not connecting the story back to the client's specific needs and data. Making the story too generic or unbelievable.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   Can you give an example of a simple story you could tell a local restaurant owner?
    *   How do you ensure your story remains data-driven and credible?
    *   How do you adapt your storytelling style for different audiences (e.g., a CEO vs. a marketing manager)?

### Question 5ï¼šImagine one of your key accounts is underperforming against its quarterly goals. What steps would you take?
*   **Points of Assessment**: Evaluates your problem-solving abilities, analytical skills, and sense of ownership. The interviewer wants to see a structured approach to diagnosing and fixing performance issues.
*   **Standard Answer**: "My first step would be to conduct a deep-dive analysis of their account to identify the root cause. I would start by reviewing changes in the competitive landscape, seasonality, and any recent alterations to their campaigns. I would then analyze key metrics like click-through rates, conversion rates, and cost per acquisition, comparing current performance to historical data. After identifying the problem areasâ€”for instance, a drop in impression share due to new competitorsâ€”I would develop a strategic optimization plan with concrete, actionable recommendations. I would then proactively schedule a meeting with the client to transparently discuss my findings and present the recovery plan. The focus would be on collaborative problem-solving and realigning our strategy to get back on track."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Jumping to conclusions without a thorough analysis. Blaming external factors without taking ownership. Failing to communicate proactively with the client.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   What if you couldn't find a clear reason for the underperformance in the data?
    *   How would you manage the client's expectations during this period?
    *   Describe a specific optimization you made that turned a campaign around.

### Question 7ï¼šHow do you stay updated on the latest trends and changes in the digital advertising industry?
*   **Points of Assessment**: This question assesses your passion for the industry, your proactivity, and your commitment to continuous learning. Google values individuals who are curious and stay ahead of the curve.
*   **Standard Answer**: "I am passionate about digital marketing and make it a priority to stay informed. I also follow key industry experts on LinkedIn and listen to marketing podcasts during my commute to get diverse perspectives. For more technical knowledge, I regularly review the official Google Ads Help Center and Skillshop courses to stay updated on new features and best practices. This combination of sources gives me a well-rounded view of both high-level strategic shifts and the tactical changes that directly impact my clients' campaigns."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Mentioning only one source or very generic sources. Not being able to name specific publications or experts. Lacking genuine enthusiasm for the subject.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   Tell me about a recent industry trend that you find particularly interesting and why.
    *   How would you apply that trend to help a small business client?
    *   How do you filter out the noise and focus on what's truly important?

### Question 8ï¼šDescribe a time you had to manage a client's expectations when their goals were unrealistic.
*   **Points of Assessment**: This question tests your client management skills, honesty, and ability to have difficult conversations constructively. The interviewer wants to know if you can be a realistic and trusted advisor.
*   **Standard Answer**: "I once had a new client who wanted to achieve a number one ranking for a highly competitive, broad keyword within their first month, on a very small budget. I knew this was not a realistic goal. I started by validating their ambition but then gently educated them on the competitive landscape using Google's own data on bid estimates and keyword difficulty. I reframed the objective from a vanity metric (ranking #1) to a business metric (generating qualified leads). I proposed a more strategic approach focusing on less competitive, long-tail keywords where they could achieve visibility and drive conversions within their budget. By shifting the conversation from an unrealistic goal to an achievable, impactful strategy, I was able to manage their expectations and set our partnership up for success."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Agreeing to the unrealistic goal just to please the client. Bluntly telling the client they are wrong without providing data or an alternative solution. Lacking empathy for the client's ambition.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   What do you do if a client is insistent on a strategy you know will fail?
    *   How do you report bad news or poor results to a client?
    *   How do you balance being an advisor with the need to meet sales goals?

### Question 9ï¼šHow would you balance the need to drive revenue for Google with providing the best possible advice for your client's success?
*   **Points of Assessment**: This is a crucial question about ethics and priorities. The interviewer wants to confirm that you understand Google's client-centric philosophy: long-term client success leads to long-term revenue for Google.
*   **Standard Answer**: "I believe that the two goals are fundamentally aligned. My primary focus is always on the client's success, because a successful client will see the value in our partnership and will be more likely to grow their investment with Google over the long term. If a certain product or higher budget isn't right for a client's current objectives, I would not recommend it, as that would erode trust and lead to poor performance and eventual churn. My strategy is to act as a trusted advisor, providing honest recommendations that drive tangible business results for my clients. This approach fosters a healthy, sustainable relationship that naturally leads to revenue growth for Google as the client's business thrives."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Suggesting that Google's revenue comes first. Seeing the two goals as being in constant conflict. Not being able to articulate the long-term vision.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   Have you ever had to recommend a client *decrease* their budget? Why?
    *   How would you handle pressure from management to push a specific product that you don't think is right for your client?
    *   How do you define "client success"?

### Question 10ï¼šWhy are you interested in working with small- and medium-sized businesses specifically?
*   **Points of Assessment**: This question explores your motivation and cultural fit. The interviewer wants to understand if you have a genuine passion for the GCS mission of helping SMBs grow.

*   **Common Pitfalls**: Giving a generic answer about wanting to work for Google. Not showing any specific empathy or understanding of the SMB segment. Sounding as if SMBs are just a stepping stone to working with larger clients.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
