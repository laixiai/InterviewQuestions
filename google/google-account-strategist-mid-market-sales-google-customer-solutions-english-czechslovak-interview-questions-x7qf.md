## 10 Typical Account Strategist, Mid-Market Sales, Google Customer Solutions (English, Czech/Slovak) Interview Questions

### Question 1ï¼šDescribe your experience managing a portfolio of clients in a sales context. How do you prioritize your efforts to drive the most growth?
*   **Points of Assessment**: The interviewer is evaluating your strategic thinking, time management, and ability to identify high-impact opportunities. They want to see if you have a systematic approach to portfolio management.
*   **Standard Answer**: "In my previous role, I managed a portfolio of 50 mid-market clients. My approach to prioritization was data-driven, based on a tiered system. I segmented clients into high, medium, and potential growth tiers based on their current spend, growth potential, and strategic importance. I dedicated the majority of my proactive outreach and strategic planning sessions to the high-growth tier. For the medium tier, I focused on consistent optimization and identifying incremental opportunities. For the potential tier, I used scalable communication methods to nurture them. This allowed me to consistently hit and exceed my growth targets by focusing my energy where it would yield the greatest return."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Giving a generic answer like "I treat all my clients equally." Failing to provide a structured, data-informed methodology for prioritization.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   How would you identify a client with high growth potential?
    *   What tools or metrics do you use to manage your portfolio?
    *   Describe a time you had to re-allocate your focus from one client to another.

### Question 2ï¼šWalk me through how you would build a multi-quarter strategic plan for a new client in the e-commerce sector.
*   **Points of Assessment**: This question assesses your strategic planning skills, business acumen, and understanding of the digital advertising landscape. The interviewer wants to see your process from discovery to execution.
*   **Standard Answer**: "First, I would conduct a deep-dive discovery session to understand their core business objectives, target audience, competitive landscape, and historical performance data. In the first quarter, the focus would be on foundational success: optimizing their Google Ads campaigns, ensuring proper conversion tracking, and establishing clear KPIs. For the second quarter, I would introduce a plan for expansion, perhaps by testing new channels like YouTube or Display, based on the data gathered. In the third and fourth quarters, the strategy would evolve to focus on scaling success and maximizing lifetime value, potentially by leveraging advanced audience segmentation and AI-powered bidding strategies, always tying back to their primary business goals."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Proposing a one-size-fits-all plan without mentioning discovery. Focusing only on short-term tactics instead of a long-term strategic vision.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   How would you identify risks to this plan?
    *   What data points would be most critical in your initial analysis?
    *   How would you present this plan to a C-level executive?

### Question 3ï¼šTell me about a time you used storytelling to persuade a skeptical client to adopt a new advertising strategy.
*   **Points of Assessment**: This evaluates your communication, persuasion, and client engagement skills, specifically your ability to build compelling narratives as mentioned in the preferred qualifications.
*   **Standard Answer**: "I once worked with a client who was hesitant to invest in video advertising, believing it was too expensive and difficult to measure. Instead of just presenting data, I built a narrative around a hypothetical customer. I described the customer's journey, showing how they discovered a similar brand through a compelling YouTube ad while researching their hobby. I supported this story with industry case studies and projected ROI figures tailored to their business. This narrative helped the client visualize the customer's experience and understand the 'why' behind the strategy, not just the 'what.' They agreed to a pilot campaign, which ended up exceeding their initial ROI goals by 30%."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Simply stating that you "told a good story" without detailing the structure or impact. Confusing a data presentation with storytelling.
*   **Potential Follow--up Questions**:
    *   What are the key elements of a compelling business narrative?
    *   How do you tailor a story to different audiences?
    *   What was the outcome of that campaign?

### Question 4ï¼šHow would you handle a situation where a key client is pushing back on your recommendations and threatening to reduce their budget?
*   **Points of Assessment**: This question tests your objection handling, negotiation skills, and ability to maintain client relationships under pressure.
*   **Standard Answer**: "My first step would be to actively listen and understand the root cause of their concern without being defensive. I would ask clarifying questions to diagnose if the issue is with campaign performance, perceived value, or external business pressures. I would then re-center the conversation on their primary business goals, acknowledging their concerns while using data to demonstrate the long-term value and potential negative impact of reducing the budget. I would offer to run a smaller-scale test of my recommendation to build their confidence or explore alternative solutions that still align with their objectives. The key is to reinforce that I am their partner, committed to their success."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Immediately becoming defensive or trying to "win" the argument. Offering a discount without understanding the core problem.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   Describe a specific time you successfully managed a client's objection.
    *   What do you do if you genuinely believe the client's preferred approach is wrong?
    *   How do you differentiate between valid concerns and simple budget negotiations?

*   **Common Pitfalls**: Giving a vague answer like "I read blogs." Mentioning only one source of information.
