## 10 Typical Account Strategist, SBS Engage, Google Customer Solutions, Baltics (Multiple Language) Interview Questions

### Question 1ï¼šDescribe a time you worked with a small business client who was skeptical about investing in digital advertising. How did you convince them of its value?
*   **Points of Assessment**: This question assesses your consultative selling skills, your ability to handle objections, and your use of data or storytelling to build a convincing argument.
*   **Standard Answer**: "I once worked with a traditional brick-and-mortar retailer who believed their local customer base couldn't be reached effectively online. I started by acknowledging their concerns and asking questions to understand their business goals, which included increasing foot traffic. Instead of pushing a complex campaign, I presented hyper-local search data showing how many users in their immediate vicinity were searching for their products. I proposed a small, geo-targeted search campaign with a modest budget. I explained we would track store visit conversions to directly measure the ROI. By focusing on their specific goal and providing a low-risk, measurable starting point, I built trust and demonstrated the value, which led to them becoming a long-term client."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Giving a generic answer about the benefits of digital advertising without tailoring it to a specific client scenario. Failing to first listen and understand the client's perspective before presenting a solution.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   What specific metrics did you use to prove the campaign's success?
    *   How did you handle their objections about the cost?
    *   How did you evolve the strategy after the initial campaign?

### Question 2ï¼šHow would you prioritize your time and effort when managing a large portfolio of small business accounts with varying needs and potential?
*   **Points of Assessment**: Evaluates your portfolio management, strategic thinking, and time management skills. The interviewer wants to see if you have a clear methodology for identifying and focusing on high-impact activities.
*   **Standard Answer**: "I would prioritize my portfolio using a tiered approach based on growth potential and current engagement. I'd segment clients into groups: high-growth potential, steady performers, and at-risk accounts. High-potential clients would receive the most proactive, in-depth strategic consultations. For steady performers, I'd focus on efficiency, using scalable solutions and regular, structured check-ins to identify incremental opportunities. For at-risk accounts, the priority would be to diagnose issues and implement retention strategies. This data-driven segmentation allows me to allocate my time most effectively to meet and exceed my overall portfolio growth targets."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Suggesting a "first-come, first-served" approach. Lacking a clear, logical framework for prioritization. Not mentioning the importance of data in the decision-making process.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   What signals would you look for to identify a "high-growth potential" account?
    *   How would you use technology or automation to manage lower-tier accounts efficiently?
    *   Describe a situation where you had to re-prioritize your portfolio due to unexpected changes.

### Question 3ï¼šA client's Google Ads campaign is underperforming. Walk me through the steps you would take to diagnose the problem and present a solution.
*   **Points of Assessment**: This question tests your analytical skills, knowledge of Google Ads, and your client communication process.
*   **Standard Answer**: "My first step would be a comprehensive audit of the account, starting with the client's original objectives. I'd analyze key metrics like CTR, conversion rate, and cost-per-acquisition, comparing them to historical data and industry benchmarks. I'd examine the campaign structure, keyword strategy, ad copy relevance, and landing page experience. For example, if I see high impressions but low clicks, I'd investigate keyword match types and ad copy. Once I've identified the likely causes, I would develop a clear optimization plan with specific, actionable recommendations and present it to the client, explaining the 'why' behind each change and setting realistic expectations for improvement."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Jumping to solutions without a thorough diagnosis. Using overly technical jargon that a small business owner might not understand. Failing to connect the proposed changes back to the client's business goals.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   What's the most common reason you've seen for underperformance in SMB campaigns?
    *   How would you communicate a necessary budget increase to the client?
    *   What if the client is resistant to your proposed changes?

### Question 4ï¼šThis role requires fluency in a Baltic language. Can you describe your experience in conducting business or building professional relationships in either Lithuanian, Latvian, or Estonian?
*   **Points of Assessment**: Directly assesses the language qualification and your ability to navigate the cultural nuances of the Baltic business environment.
*   **Standard Answer**: "In my previous role at [Company], I was responsible for the [Lithuanian/Latvian/Estonian] market. All of my client communication, from initial outreach calls and emails to formal business pitches and contract negotiations, was conducted in [Language]. I understand the importance of building personal rapport and trust within the [Baltic Country] business culture. This experience has made me adept at not just translating business concepts, but also communicating them in a way that is culturally resonant and effective for building strong, lasting client partnerships in the region."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Simply stating fluency without providing concrete examples of its professional application. Underestimating the importance of cultural context in business communication.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   Can you give an example of a business concept that requires careful phrasing in [Language]?
    *   What are some key differences you've observed in business communication styles between the Baltic region and other regions?
    *   How do you stay current with business terminology in [Language]?

### Question 5ï¼šHow do you stay up-to-date with the latest trends in digital marketing and changes to Google's advertising products?
*   **Points of Assessment**: Measures your proactivity, commitment to continuous learning, and passion for the industry.
*   **Standard Answer**: "I'm committed to continuous learning and stay current through multiple channels. I regularly follow Google's official advertising blog and Skillshop for product updates and certifications. I also subscribe to industry publications like Search Engine Land and listen to marketing podcasts to understand broader trends and competitive strategies. Furthermore, I believe in learning from data, so I often run small-scale experiments in test accounts to understand the real-world impact of new features. This combination of official sources, industry analysis, and hands-on practice ensures my advice is both current and effective."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Mentioning only one source (e.g., "I read a blog sometimes"). Not connecting the learning back to how it benefits clients. Appearing passive in your learning approach.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   Tell me about a recent industry trend that you believe is particularly relevant for SMBs.
    *   How would you explain a complex product update, like the move to Performance Max, to a non-technical client?
    *   Which digital marketing thought leader do you follow and why?

### Question 6ï¼šImagine you're tasked with achieving a 20% growth target for your portfolio this quarter. What would be your strategy?
*   **Points of Assessment**: Evaluates your strategic planning, goal-orientation, and ability to translate a target into an actionable plan.
*   **Standard Answer**: "To achieve a 20% growth target, I'd first analyze my portfolio to identify the biggest opportunities. I would segment my clients to find those with the highest potential for budget increases or expansion into new Google products. My strategy would be two-pronged: upselling and cross-selling. For upselling, I would focus on high-performing accounts, presenting data-driven cases for increasing their investment. For cross-selling, I'd identify clients using only Search ads and pitch the benefits of adding YouTube or Display to reach new audiences. I would meticulously track my progress against the target weekly and adjust my focus based on performance to ensure I stay on track."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Providing a vague answer like "I'll just work harder." Not breaking down the target into specific, actionable steps. Forgetting the importance of tracking and adjusting the strategy.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   What would you do if you were halfway through the quarter and behind on your target?
    *   How would you balance pursuing new growth with retaining existing clients?
    *   What data would you use to build a case for a client to increase their budget?

### Question 7ï¼šTell me about a time you had to manage a client's expectations when they had unrealistic goals.
*   **Points of Assessment**: Assesses your communication skills, honesty, and ability to build trust even when delivering difficult news.
*   **Standard Answer**: "I had a new client who wanted to become the top search result for a highly competitive keyword within a week on a very small budget. I started by validating their ambition but then gently educated them on the realities of the ad auction and keyword competition. I used Google's Keyword Planner to show them the estimated bids and competition level. Instead of saying 'no,' I proposed a more realistic, strategic alternative: focusing on less competitive, long-tail keywords where they could dominate and see immediate ROI. This approach built credibility and set the foundation for a trusting, long-term partnership based on realistic, shared goals."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Being confrontational or simply telling the client they are wrong. Agreeing to unrealistic goals that you know you cannot meet. Lacking a constructive alternative to the client's initial request.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   How did the client react initially?
    *   What was the outcome of the alternative strategy you proposed?
    *   How do you proactively set expectations at the beginning of a client relationship?

### Question 8ï¼šThis job involves a high volume of phone calls and emails. How do you maintain energy and provide a high-quality, personalized experience for every client?
*   **Points of Assessment**: Checks for resilience, efficiency, and a customer-centric mindset in a high-paced sales environment.
*   **Standard Answer**: "I thrive in a fast-paced environment and stay motivated by focusing on the impact I'm having on my clients' businesses. To stay efficient, I block my time for different tasks, such as proactive outreach, follow-ups, and strategic planning. Before each call, I take a few minutes to review the client's account and my notes to ensure the conversation is personalized and adds value. I use CRM tools diligently to keep track of all interactions. Maintaining a positive and helpful attitude is key; I see every interaction as a new opportunity to strengthen the relationship and help them succeed."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Admitting you find high-volume work draining. Lacking a system for organization. Suggesting that you would provide a generic experience to some clients to save time.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   How do you handle a day when you face multiple rejections from clients?
    *   What tools or techniques do you use to stay organized?
    *   Describe your approach to preparing for a client call.

### Question 9ï¼šHow would you use storytelling in a sales pitch to a small business owner?
*   **Points of Assessment**: Probes the "Preferred Qualification" of building compelling narratives and your ability to connect with clients on an emotional level.
*   **Standard Answer**: "Instead of just listing product features, I would use storytelling to make the benefits tangible. For example, if I'm talking to a local bakery owner, I would find a case study of a similar business. I'd tell the story of how that bakery was struggling to attract new customers and then used geo-targeted search ads to appear when people searched 'best cupcakes near me.' I would describe their success not just in clicks, but in the real-world outcome of selling out of their specialty cupcake on a weekend. This narrative approach makes the solution relatable and helps the business owner envision their own success."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Confusing storytelling with simply talking a lot. Telling a story that isn't relevant to the client's specific business or goals. Failing to back up the story with relevant data points.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   Where would you find the information to create such a story?
    *   What are the key elements of a good business story?
    *   How do you ensure your story is authentic and credible?

### Question 10ï¼šWhy are you interested in working with small and medium-sized businesses specifically, as opposed to large corporations?
*   **Points of Assessment**: Assesses your motivation for the role and your alignment with the mission of the Google Customer Solutions team.
*   **Standard Answer**: "I'm specifically drawn to working with SMBs because of the direct and tangible impact I can have on their success. With large corporations, you're often a small part of a massive marketing machine. With SMBs, you're frequently advising the owner directly, and the success of an advertising campaign can mean hiring a new employee or even opening a second location. I find it incredibly rewarding to be a true partner in their growth journey, helping the backbone of our communities thrive in the digital age. This role provides a unique opportunity to make that kind of meaningful difference."
*   **Common Pitfalls**: Suggesting that SMBs are just a stepping stone to working with larger clients. Lacking a genuine passion for the SMB segment. Giving a generic answer about wanting to work for Google.
*   **Potential Follow-up Questions**:
    *   What do you think is the biggest marketing challenge facing SMBs today?
    *   How does your approach differ when speaking to a business owner versus a marketing manager?
    *   What does "customer success" mean to you in the context of an SMB?
